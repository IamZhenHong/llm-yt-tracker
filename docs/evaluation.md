## 4. Topic Precision (LLM-as-judge, two-pass)

| Label | Count | Share |
|---|---|---|
| correct (semantic match) | 8 | 12.1% |
| partial (related, narrower/broader) | 25 | 37.9% |
| wrong (not in the judge's list) | 33 | 50.0% |

**Strict precision:** 8/66 = **12.1%**.
**Precision including partials:** 33/66 = **50.0%**.

**Reading.** The strict number looks bad. The nuance: the judge was instructed to propose 3–5 topics characterizing the video, while the extractor can produce up to 5 topics — including *specific* canonical names from the prompt-provided list (e.g. "RLHF", "mixture of experts", "quantization"). Many extracted topics are finer-grained than anything the judge proposed, so they land in `partial` or `wrong` even when they're arguably correct.

Example from `s6H4QF5K9MM`:
- Extractor: `["GPT-5.5", "OpenAI", "AI coding", "agentic models", "workplace productivity"]`
- Judge's cold topics: `["AI model launches", "coding assistants", "model benchmarks"]`
- Grading: 5 wrong — but "AI coding" and "agentic models" are clearly reflected in the transcript, just not in the judge's particular 3-topic summary.

**The 50% partial-or-better number is probably the fairer read.** Tightening this would require either a larger judge topic list per pass or a LLM-judge with semantic-similarity grading rather than strict list matching. Deferred as future work.

- **Topic precision grading is strict by construction.** See the reading above; the 50% partial-or-better figure is more charitable and probably closer to user-felt quality.
- **Supadata fallback was triggered for 6/12 videos on day one** due to YouTube IP flagging. Whether that share stays elevated in steady state depends on YouTube's per-IP thresholds and the size of future backfills.
